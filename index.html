Bipolar I distinction

This type of bipolar can only be diagnosed with evidence of at least one manic episode. A hypomanic or depressive episode MAY also occur.

What is a manic episode

ANSWER

  • Emotional: Distinct period of mood change (e.g., elevated, expansive, or irritable) that seems abnormal.
  • Behavioural: Persistent and abnormal increase in energy or goal-directed behaviour
  • Both changes must last at least 1 week, be present most days for most of the day.
  • 3 or more of these symptoms are present during mood change:
    1. Inflated self-esteem
    2. Less need for sleep
    3. More talkative
    4. Racing thoughts
    5. Easily distracted
    6. Increase in activity (goal-directed or purposeless)
    7. Risky/impulsive behaviour
  • Causes marked impairment in functioning, or requires hospitalisation, or psychotic features
  • Not attributable to medication

What is a hypomanic episode

Identical to a manic episode, but of shorter duration (4 days as opposed to 1 week). And it is NOT severe enough to cause marked impairment or warrant hospitalisation

Bipolar II distinction

This type of bipolar includes the presence of both a hypomanic episode and a depressive episode (current or past), and NO manic episodes.

What is a major depressive episode (MDE; bipolar context)

ANSWER

  • 5 or more of the following are present for 2 weeks (must include 1 of the first 2):
    1. Depressed mood most of the day
    2. Lack of pleasure/interest
    3. Weight loss or gain
    4. Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day
    5. Psychomotor agitation or retardation
    6. Fatigue nearly every day
    7. Worthlessness or guilt
    8. Concentration problems
    9. Recurrent thoughts of death
  • Causes significant distress or impairment in functioning
  • Not attributable to medication or other condition

BPD criteria

ANSWER

5 or more of the following:

  • Frantic efforts to avoid abandonment
  • Pattern of unstable and intense relationships alternating between idealisation and devaluation
  • Unstable self-image
  • Dangerous impulsivity in atleast two areas (excluding suicidality and self-mutilating)
  • Recurrent suicidality or self-mutilation
  • Affective instability/reactive mood
  • Chronic feelings of emptiness
  • Inappropriate anger or violence
  • Stress-related Paranoia or dissociation

Rumination disorder definition

A disorder that involves the repeated regurgitation of food for atleast 1 month. Not associated with another medical condition or eating disorder.

ANSWER:
 Disturbed eating behaviour that includes 1 or more of the following:

  • not meeting appropriate nutritional needs leading to weight-loss
  • not meeting expected weight-gain in children
  • nutritional deficiency
  • dependence on enteral feeding (tube)
  • interference with psychosocial functioning.

Avoidant/restrictive food intake disorder definition

An eating disturbance (e.g., apparent lack of interest in eating or food; avoidance based on sensory characteristics of food; concern about aversive consequences of eating) as manifested by persistent failure to meet appropriate nutritional and/or energy needs

Anorexia nervosa definition

Which eating disorder?

  • Restriction of food resulting in low body weight.
  • Fear of gaining weight, despite low body weight
  • Distorted perception of body weight or shape OR lack of recognition of seriousness of low body weight

Bulimia nervosa definition

Which eating disorder?

  • Weight is normal or above normal
  • Eating an abnormally large amount of food in a short (<2hours) period. Feeling out of control
  • Behaviours to avoid weight gain (vomiting, laxatives/meds, fasting, excessive exercise)
  • Binging and fasting both occur at least once a week for 3 months
  • Self-evaluation unduly influenced by weight

Two subtypes of anorexia nervosa

Restricting subtype (not purging) and binge-eating/purging subtype.

Main distinction between anorexia and bulimia

ANSWER:

Anorexic people maintain low body weight and may or may not binge/purge, bulimic people are at least normal weight and must binge/purge.

OCD: define obsessions

Thoughts, urges, or images that are experienced as intrusive/unwanted. The person tries to either supress it or neutralise it through compulsions.

OCD: define compulsions

Repetitive behaviours or mental acts that the person feels driven to perform in response to an obsession or rigid rules. The acts are aimed at reducing anxiety/distress or preventing a dreaded outcome. However, the acts are not connected to the outcome the aim to achieve, or are clearly excessive.

OCD criteria:

ANSWER:

  • Presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both.
  • Obsessions and/or compulsions are time consuming (1 hour+ per day), or cause distress or functional impairment.
  • Normal exclusions (substances, other disorders)
  • Specify level of insight (or delusional beliefs)

Define: Somatic Symptom and related disorders

A group of disorders that include prominent somatic symptoms that cause distress and impairment. The symptoms may or may not be medically explained, but they are paired with abnormal behaviours, feelings and thoughts.

 

Somatic Symptom Disorder Criteria

ANSWER:

  • 1 or more somatic symptoms that result in disruption of daily life
  • Excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviours related to the somatic symptoms in 1 of the following ways:
    1. Disproportionate thoughts about the seriousness of symptoms.
    2. High levels of health anxiety
    3. Excessive time spent on symptoms or health concerns
  • Symptom(s) last (not necessarily consistently) for more than 6 months.
  • Specify whether there is prominent pain, and severity.

Panic Disorder Criteria

A disorder involving: unexpected and recurrent panic attacks.

  • At least 1 panic attack is followed by at least 1 month of either:
    1. worry about additional panic attacks
    2. or behaviour changes to avoid panic attacks
  • Normal exclusions

Panic attack description/criteria (panic disorder)

ANSWER:

  • Abrupt surge of intense fear/discomfort that peaks within minutes, during which time 4 or more of the following occur:
    1. Heart change (rate, palpitations).
    2. Sweating
    3. Shaking
    4. Breathing difficulty
    5. Feeling of choking
    6. Chest pain
    7. Nausea
    8. Light-headedness
    9. Chills/heat
    10. Paraesthesia (numb)
    11. Derealization
    12. Fear of losing control or going crazy
    13. Fear of dying

PTSD definition/criteria

A disorder involving exposure to a traumatic event coupled with intrusive recollections, avoidant behaviours, mood/thought changes, and increased reactivity lasting more than 1 month after the event or its consequences.

 

Specify:

  • With dissociative symptoms? (depersonalisation, derealization)
  • Delayed expression (symptoms began > 6 months after event)

PTSD traumatic event criteria

ANSWER:

  • Must have included actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence.
  • Exposure to the event in 1 of these ways:
    1. Direct (happened to me)
    2. Witnessing (happened to other)
    3. Learning that it happened to close person (family usually)
    4. Repeated or extreme exposure to details of the event (police, ambulance)

PTSD intrusive symptom criteria

ANSWER:

  • Recurrent, involuntary, intrusive distressing memory of the event
  • Recurrent distressing dreams about the event
  • Dissociative reactions (flashbacks. Feeling as if you are back in the event)
  • Psychological or physiological distress in reaction to internal or external cues that symbolise/resemble the event

PTSD avoidance symptom criteria

ANSWER:

1 or both of the following:

  • Avoiding memories, thoughts, or feelings about the event
  • Avoiding external reminders of the event

PTSD alteration in mood/thinking criteria

ANSWER:

2 or more of the following:

  • Inability remembering important aspects of the event
  • Persistent, exaggerated negative beliefs about self, others, or the world.
  • Persistent distorted thoughts about the causes or consequences of the event
  • Persistent negative state (e.g., horror, anger, shame)
  • Diminished interest or participation in important activities
  • Feeling detached or estranged from others
  • Persistent inability to experience positive emotions

PTSD arousal/reactivity symptom criteria
  • Irritable, and angry outbursts
  • Reckless/self-destructive behaviour
  • Hypervigilance
  • Exaggerated startle response
  • Problems with concentration
  • Problems with sleep

Schizophrenia criteria

Present for a 1 month period:

  • 1 or more positive symptoms (delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized speech)
  • Disorganized or catatonic behaviour
  • Negative symptoms (reduced emotional expression, avolition)

Separation anxiety criteria:

ANSWER:

Fear or anxiety in the face of separation from attachment figures that is excessive or developmentally inappropriate. At least 3 of the following are present for 4 weeks (children) or 6 months (adults):

  • Distress when anticipating the separation.
  • Excessive worry about safety of attachment figure
  • Worry about events that could cause separation.
  • Refusal to go places due to fear of separation.
  • excessive fear of being alone (without attachment figure)
  • refusal to sleep away from attachment figure.
  • nightmares with separation themes
  • physical symptoms related to separation (e.g., headache, nausea)

Social Anxiety criteria

ANSWER:

  • Fear of social situations in which one may be exposed to scrutiny.
  • Fear that anxiety will be evident, and judged negatively
  • Anxiety in relevant social situations is fairly consistent and lasts 6+ months
  • Relevant social situations are either avoided or endured with intense anxiety.
  • Fear is out of proportion to the threat
  • Specify if performance related

 

Substance use disorders

A group of disorders involving cognitive, behavioural, and physical symptoms that indicate continued use in the face of significant substance related problems. Symptoms vary among substances (10 classes of substance), but include:

  • Impaired control over use
  • Social or occupational impairment
  • Risky use. Using despite knowing danger
  • Building tolerance to the substance
  • Withdrawal symptoms

Key features of Acetylcholine (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter involved with learning, memory, and attention

Key features of Epinephrine (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter secreted by endocrine glands above the kidneys. AKA adrenaline. Regulates fight or fight response.

Key features of Norepinephrine (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter related to wakefulness and alertness

Key features of Dopamine (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter related to behavioural regulation, movement, learning, mood, and attention. Can be both excitatory and inhibitory

Key features of Serotonin (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter related to inhibition of activity and behaviour. Relevant for mood regulation, appetite, and sleep.

Key features of GABA (neurotransmitter)

Neurotransmitter that is inhibitory and can produce stability by decreasing neural transmission. Barbiturates and benzodiazepines increase GABA.

Two types of reports

ANSWER:

Treatment report, or a third-party report

What is a treatment report?

A type of report that assists in the treatment of a client, such as a referral to a psychiatrist, or a review sent to the GP

What is a third-party report?

A report that is not about treatment and is instead about communicating with third-parties for other reasons, like legal or money reasons. E.g., explaining to a court why a client did what they did.
